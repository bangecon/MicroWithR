In-Kind versus Cash Transfers

Economists claim that cash transfers are more ‚Äúefficient‚Äù than in-kind transfers because there is no dead-weigth utility loss from the fact that the recipient might have spent the money differently.1

Initial Solution

Consider a consumer with income of 100 (\(m = 100\)), which they can allocate by consuming necessities (e.g.¬†food, housing) or invseting in capital (e.g.¬†home production, education) at prices price (\(p_n = 1\) and \(p_k = 2\)). Also suppose that the consumer has preferences dictated by a constant elasticity of substitution utility function given by:

\[u(q_n, q_k) = (0.1q_n^{1/2} + 0.9q_k^{1/2})^2\]

This makes the consumer‚Äôs maximization problem:

\[max \big\{(0.1q_n^{1/2} + 0.9q_k^{1/2})^2\big\}\] subject to:

\[1q_n + 2q_k \le 100\]

Find the initial solution to the consumer‚Äôs utility-maximization problem. Print the utility maximizing consumption choice (pars) for the solution.
